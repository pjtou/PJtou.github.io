Samkhya Foundation in Astrology

Pierre Touchard, Jan. 2018

Egyptian Astrology and Philosophy

In what is called western astrology, which is more like the remnant of Egyptian/Mesopotamian/Persian astrology from the middle east transmitted to us via the Arabs, and then via European astrologers of the middle age, there is no discernible philosophical foundation, that this writer is aware of.   Ptolemy and Vettius Valens, in the 2nd century AD, make an effort to link Aristotelian ideas with Astrology, and also for Valens, to Stoic ideas.   For example, we find the 4 Elements theory in Ptolemy and Valens,  discussed by Aristotle, and then the Stoics.   But Aristotle was not an astrologer, and he did he leave any astrological writings.   So it seems more likely that Ptolemy was trying to find a philosophical support using Aristotle, in order to express an astrological system with a philosophical foundation.   It does not seem very clear from the books and literature, in Dorotheus de Sidon, 1th Century AD, not Ptolemy and Valens, that astrology and its cosmology has any particular philosophy underlying it.   Indeed, it seems to me very strongly that these astrologers around the 1st century AD in Alexandria, before and after the time of JC actually, were trying to understand books and ideas that were long gone.   Vettius Valens was travelling far and wide trying to find the source and secrets of an astrology whose source was in the distant past.  


Indian Astrology and Sankhya

Indian astrology on the contrary, is based on a philosohical system, or at least it uses philosophical ideas with enormous roots in Indian philosophy, and this is Samkhya.  The Indian astrology books of the 20th century do not stress this much, but in the 1980s and 90s R. Santhanam and Kapoor produced their english translation of Brihat Parasara Hora Shastra, by Rishi Parasara, followed by the translation of G Sharma, and it became the foremost authoritative book, in and outside of India,  and the most respected.   Parasara was long known to be one of the 18 Rishis (Sages) who expounded Astrology for mankind.  And the first few chapters of BPHS DO contain a delineation of astrological ideas in terms of Shankhya.  Most importantly the planets are understood from the Gunas and Doshas.   These are clear Shankhya notions that are discussed in the root text of Shankhya by Kapila, and also discussed at length in the Upanishads, the Bhagavad Gita, and the Puranas.  

Shankhya and Indian philosophy in general, are difficult texts to approach from a westerner's point of view.   Shankhya describes a world whose origin is not material.   It is often descrived as a dualistic philosophy, because it holds 2 realities as basic principles of the world, Purusha and Prakriti.  Of these, Purusha is seen as Self, or Spirit, or Consciousness.  Some writers have seen Purusha as synonimous with Atman, or Soul.  Purusha is a divine essence, it is spiritual.  At any rate, Purusha being Consciousness or Spirit is totally immaterial.   And this is a very important notion, because Purusha being immaterial means it is NOT perceivable by the senses !   On the contrary Prakriti is nature, or matter, the the 5 senses do have access to a large part of nature.  Shankhya proceeds to decribe Prakriti at length with its 24 tattvas, or true principles, the last tattva the 25th being Purusha itself, but this one is transcendant, meaning not perceivable by the Senses.  

So these Sankhya concepts forming a description of what ancient Sages have SEEN of the world, with its origin being NOT perceivable by the Senses, we can see how our modern world will have difficulty accepting such a point of view.   All philosophical systems born in the west since the Renaissance, and even before, are reasonned out processes and intellectual constructions.   All modern sciences are attempting to describe what they see in matter and nature, and from that extrapolate to what the world must be like.  They use instruments to refine what the senses fail to see, microscopes, telescope.    Still they are unable to locate consciousness, love,  God, Spirit, within matter.   They begin their inquiry from the opposite side of where the Shankhya Rishis started out.  Those Rishis worked from yogic consciousness which they elaborated on in Vedic texts.   They explain that once their mind where cleared of obstructions thru their meditative disciplines, they were able to see the world, which they describe in Shankhya.   To us then, who cannot have access to expanded consciousness, Shankhya seems speculation, but to a Rishi who SEES the world, modern Science, by looking only at Prakriti, nature, is necessarily blind, hence speculation.
